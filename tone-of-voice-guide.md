# Tone of Voice Guide

**Site:** Personal Finance Education  
**Author:** Solo Creator  
**Last updated:** March 2026

---

## The one-sentence summary

Write like a knowledgeable friend who respects the reader's intelligence — warm, direct, and always useful.

---

## Core principles

### 1. Personal, not casual
The site is written by one person. That should feel like a strength, not a limitation. Use "I" when sharing a perspective or recommendation, but keep the focus on "you" — the reader. The reader's situation is always the subject. Yours is just the context.

> ✅ *"I find it more useful to compare two scenarios side by side, rather than chase a single 'right' number."*  
> ❌ *"We recommend comparing two scenarios."* — sounds corporate  
> ❌ *"Comparing scenarios is recommended."* — sounds lifeless

---

### 2. Friendly, not fluffy
Warmth comes from clarity and respect, not from exclamation marks or hollow encouragement. Never pad a sentence to sound approachable. A short, clear sentence is friendlier than a long, enthusiastic one.

> ✅ *"That one-point difference matters more than most people expect."*  
> ❌ *"Great news — small changes can make a huge difference!"*

---

### 3. Professional, not stiff
Professional means trustworthy, accurate, and well-structured — not formal or distant. Contractions are fine. Plain language is always preferred over financial jargon. If a technical term is needed, define it immediately and move on.

> ✅ *"Real return is simply what's left after inflation takes its share."*  
> ❌ *"Real return refers to the inflation-adjusted rate of portfolio appreciation."*

---

### 4. Genuinely helpful, not optimistic by default
Finance content often softens hard truths to avoid discouraging readers. Do the opposite. A useful answer is more trustworthy than a flattering one. Be honest about uncertainty, trade-offs, and the limits of any single number or model.

> ✅ *"This calculator can't predict the future — but it can help you make a decision you'll feel good about either way."*  
> ❌ *"Use this tool to plan your path to financial freedom!"*

---

## Voice at a glance

| Trait | Do | Don't |
|---|---|---|
| Perspective | "I" for opinions, "you" for focus | "We" — there is no we |
| Warmth | Comes from clarity and directness | Exclamation marks, hype, filler phrases |
| Expertise | Shown through explanation, not labels | "As an expert…", credential-dropping |
| Tone | Steady, calm, encouraging | Alarmist, salesy, or overly cautious |
| Sentences | Short and varied | Long chains of clauses |

## SEO writing rules

These rules serve both search engines and readers. Good SEO and good writing are not in conflict here — clarity wins on both fronts.

### Structure every article clearly
- Use one H1 per page (the article title)
- Use H2s for major sections, H3s for sub-points
- Keep headings descriptive and specific — they are read by both humans and crawlers

### Lead with the point
Search engines reward content that answers the query quickly. So does the reader. Put the most important idea in the first paragraph, then support it. Do not build to a conclusion.

> ✅ *"A one-point difference in return assumption can change a 30-year outcome dramatically."*  
> ❌ *"Many people wonder about return assumptions. In this article, we will explore…"*

### Use natural language for keywords
Write for the question a real person types, not a keyword string. If the topic is "long-term return assumptions," write the way someone would actually ask about it:

> "What return should I use for my retirement calculator?"  
> "Is 7% a realistic long-term investment return?"

Use the target phrase naturally in the first 100 words, at least one H2, and the meta description. Do not repeat it unnaturally.

### Keep paragraphs short
Two to four sentences per paragraph. One idea per paragraph. White space is not wasted space — it makes the page easier to read and easier to scan.

### Write descriptive meta descriptions
Every article needs a meta description of 140–160 characters. It should answer the question the article covers, in plain language, with a quiet reason to click.

> ✅ *"Learn how to choose a realistic return assumption for long-term planning — and why using a range beats relying on one number."*  
> ❌ *"Read our article about return assumptions for investors."*

### Link with intention
Internal links should use descriptive anchor text that tells the reader (and search engine) exactly what they will find. Avoid "click here" or "read more."

> ✅ *"See how nominal and real returns compare in practice."*  
> ❌ *"Click here for more."*

## Words and phrases to avoid

| Avoid | Because |
|---|---|
| "We" | There is no team — use "I" or write reader-first |
| "Simply" / "Just" | Can feel dismissive of genuine complexity |
| "Obviously" / "Of course" | Alienates readers who don't already know |
| "Financial freedom" | Overused, vague, and slightly breathless |
| "Game-changer" | Signals hype over substance |
| "In today's world" / "In this day and age" | Filler with no meaning |
| Passive voice where avoidable | Weakens trust and clarity |

## A quick self-check before publishing

Before any piece of copy goes live, ask:

1. **Would I say this to a smart friend who asked me this question?**
2. **Does the first paragraph answer the main question, or does it circle around it?**
3. **Have I used "we" anywhere? (Change to "I" or rewrite reader-first.)**
4. **Is every heading specific enough to stand alone out of context?**
5. **Would a reader feel more confident after reading this, without feeling sold to?**

If yes to all five — it's ready.
