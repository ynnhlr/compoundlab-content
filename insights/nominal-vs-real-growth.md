Nominal returns tell you how the account balance grows. Real returns tell you what that balance can still buy. Both matter, but they answer different questions.

## Why nominal growth looks better

If a calculator shows a portfolio reaching a large round number decades in the future, the result can feel more meaningful than it really is. Inflation quietly changes the standard for what counts as enough.

That is why a nominal balance should rarely be the final answer on its own. It shows progress, but not purchasing power.

## Real growth is the planning lens

Real return analysis answers a harder and more useful question: how much of the ending value survives inflation?

- Nominal growth is useful for tracking the account
- Real growth is useful for comparing future spending power
- Looking at both together helps investors avoid overconfidence

## A simple way to think about it

An investor who sees a strong future value may assume the plan is on track. But if inflation remains elevated for a long stretch, the plan may still fall short of the lifestyle or spending target that inspired the calculation in the first place.

That does not make the nominal result wrong. It makes it incomplete.

## What a better calculator should show

A stronger portfolio tool should help you compare:

1. Nominal ending balance
2. Inflation-adjusted ending balance
3. The gap between the two

Seeing that gap early leads to better contribution decisions, more realistic goals, and fewer surprises later.
